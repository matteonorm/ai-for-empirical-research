## Draft Summary (DRAFT, pending Paul)

Writing is thinking, and offloading too much of it to an LLM means offloading
the thinking itself. This video explores where AI assistance helps (boilerplate,
first-pass documentation, structured revision plans) and where it hurts
(homogenizing your voice, replacing the judgment calls that sharpen arguments).
The practical workflow: build a style guide so the LLM approximates your voice,
use the strategic-revision skill to turn referee reports into prioritized task
lists, and ask for comments on your drafts rather than rewrites so you stay in
the driver's seat.

## What you'll learn (from post's "Key takeaways" section)

Paul provides six numbered takeaways. All preserved.

Live bullets (6):
1. Writing is thinking: be deliberate about what you offload versus what you do yourself, and check whether you can discuss the ideas in depth without the LLM
2. Build a style guide (a markdown skill file) so the LLM approximates your voice, but expect it to be a useful caricature, not a perfect match
3. Start with banal writing: boilerplate, first-pass summaries, and routine documentation are where AI assistance pays off most
4. Use the strategic-revision skill to turn referee reports into a dependency-aware revision plan with prioritized execution blocks
5. Ask for comments, not rewrites: this keeps you engaged and prevents your prose from drifting toward generic LLM voice
6. Think first, then write with AI: iterate your ideas as far as you can before involving the LLM, because specificity is enormously valuable
